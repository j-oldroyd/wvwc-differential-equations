    Section 4.3 Phase Portraits and Critical Points

    The techniques used in Section 1.6 to study long-term behavior of solutions near critical points can be adapted to higher dimensional systems as well. The main difference now is that we must consider the phase plane instead of a simple one-dimensional number line. This also allows for different types of behavior at critical points for solutions, or trajectories, near the critical point.

    Subsection The Phase Plane

    Just as we were able to plot direction fields for first-order autonomous ODEs, we can do something similar for autonomous first-order systems with two equations and constant coefficients. These are precisely the systems that can be written as a matrix ODE of the form
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}^\prime = A\vec{x} \end{equation*}
    where \(A\) is a \(2\times2\) matrix.
    Consider the first-order system
    \begin{align} x^\prime_{1} \amp = a_{11}x_{1} + a_{12}x_{2}\tag{4.1}\\ x^\prime_{2} \amp = a_{21}x_{1}+ a_{22}x_{2}\notag \end{align}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \vec{x}^\prime = A\vec{x}\quad\text{where}\quad A = \begin{bmatrix}a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The solution of this system looks like \(\vec{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix}x_{1}(t) \\ x_{2}(t)\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    As \(t\) varies, \(\vec{x}(t)\) will trace out a curve in the \(x_{1}x_{2}\)-plane, which we call a trajectory. The \(x_{1}x_{2}\)-plane is called the phase plane, and the collection of all trajectories of the system (4.1) is called the phase portrait of the system. The phase portrait of a system provides us with a way to study the behavior of solutions of (4.1) without actually solving the system.

    Example 4.3.1. Sketching a phase portrait.

    Sketch a phase portrait for the system
    \begin{align*} \dv{y_{1}}{t} \amp = 2y_{1} - 3y_{2}\\ \dv{y_{2}}{t} \amp = -2y_{1} + y_{2}. \end{align*}
    Solution.
    First, note that we can rewrite the system as \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\) using
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}y_{1}\\y_{2}\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{and}\quad A = \begin{bmatrix}2 \amp -3 \\-2 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, we can view \(\vec{y}\) as corresponding to a point in the phase plane. Hence \(\vec{y}^\prime\) corresponds to a tangent of a trajectory passing through the point \(\vec{y}\text{.}\)
    For example, let’s find the tangent at the point \(\vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}2\\2\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) The tangent is given by the corresponding \(\vec{y}^\prime\) at this point which is just \(A\vec{y}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}2 \amp -3 \\ -2 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-2 \\ -2\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So at the point \((2,2)\) in the phase plane, the trajectory should be heading in the same direction as that of the point \((-2,-2)\) relative to the origin. In other words, the tangent vector would point two units left and two units down from the point \((2,2)\text{.}\)
    Similarly, if we let \(\vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}\) then we get
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}2 \amp -3 \\ -2 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-3 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So the trajectory going through \((0,1)\) in the phase plane should be heading in the direction of \((-3,1)\) viewed from the origin.
    Plotting other points in the phase plane like this, we get Figure 4.3.2. One thing we can see from this is that trajectories that lie on the line, equivalently, those with initial conditions \(y_{1}(0) = y_{2}(0)\text{,}\) appear to approach the origin while all others move away from the origin. We can see why this is by looking at the general solution of the original system, which is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y} = c_{1}e^{4t}\begin{bmatrix}-3\\2\end{bmatrix}+c_{2}e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If \(\vec{y}\) lies on the line \(y_{2} = y_{1}\text{,}\) then \(c_{1}\) has to equal \(0\text{,}\) which follows from the fact that \(\begin{bmatrix}-3\\2\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\) are linearly independent. So trajectories that lie on the line \(y_{2} = y_{1}\) must take the form \(\vec{y} = c_{2}e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) and every solution of this form goes to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Every other trajectory will move away from the origin as \(t\to\infty\text{,}\) although the trajectories that lie on the line \(y_{2} = -\frac{2}{3}y_{1}\) will travel to the origin as \(t\to-\infty\) (i.e. “backwards in time”):

    Note that \(\vec{x}=\vec{0}\) is always a solution of \(\vec{x}^\prime=A\vec{x}\text{.}\) This is because \(\vec{0}^\prime=A\vec{0} = \vec{0}\text{.}\) We call \(\vec{x} = \vec{0}\) the equilibrium solution or critical point of the system \(\vec{x}^\prime=A\vec{x}\text{.}\) Later in this section, we will be concerned with the behavior of trajectories of the system \(\vec{x}^\prime=A\vec{x}\) near the equilibrium solution \(\vec{x}=\vec{0}\text{.}\) One thing we will see is that the behavior is determined in large part by the eigenvalues of the matrix \(A\text{.}\)
    We will separate the behavior of trajectories at the critical point \(\vec{x} = \vec{0}\) into five different cases:
    \(\vec{0}\) was a saddle point in Example 4.3.1 since there were incoming trajectories on the line \(y_{2} = y_{1}\) and outgoing trajectories on the line \(y_{2} = -\frac{2}{3}y_{1}\) as indicated in Figure 4.3.2.

    Example 4.3.4. Classifying a critical point using a phase portrait.

    Using a phase portrait, determine the type of critical point that \(\vec{y} = \vec{0}\) is for the matrix ODE \(\vec{y}^\prime=A\vec{y}\) where
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}y_{1}(t) \\ y_{2}(t)\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{and}\quad A = \begin{bmatrix}3 \amp 4 \\ -4 \amp 3\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    As seen in Figure 4.3.5, every (nonzero) trajectory will spiral outward from \(\vec{y} = \vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{,}\) so \(\vec{0}\) is a spiral point of this system. To see why, we only need to look at the eigenvalues of \(A\text{,}\) which we find to be
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_{1} = 3+4i,\lambda_{2} = 3-4i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This means that the general solution of \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\) must look like
    \begin{align*} \vec{y} \amp = c_{1}e^{(3+4i)t}\vec{y}_{1} + c_{2}e^{(3-4i)t}\vec{y}_{2}\\ \amp = e^{3t}\brackets{c_{1}e^{4it}\vec{y}_{1}+c_{2}e^{-4it}\vec{y}_{2}}\\ \amp = e^{3t}\brackets{(\cos4t)\vec{x}_{1}+(\sin4t)\vec{x}_{2}}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The real part of the eigenvalues leads to the “growth term” of \(e^{3t}\) appearing in the solution, which causes the trajectories to diverge as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) The imaginary part of the eigenvalues leads to the “oscillating terms” of \(\cos4t,\sin4t\) appearing in the solution, which gives the trajectories their spiral motion.
    In general, the eigenvalues of the matrix \(A\) in the system \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\) will determine the type of critical point that \(\vec{0}\) is for the system \(\vec{y}^\prime=A\vec{y}\text{.}\)

    Example 4.3.6. Classifying trajectories algebraically.

    What kind of critical point is \(\vec{y} = \vec{0}\) for the system
    \begin{align*} y^\prime_{1} \amp = -4y_{2}\\ y^\prime_{2} \amp = 4y_{1} \end{align*}
    where \(y_{i} = y_{i}(t)\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    We could sketch the phase portrait for this system, but we can also determine the behavior of the trajectories \(\vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix} y_{1} \\ y_{2} \end{bmatrix} \) if we can find a relationship between \(y_{1}\) and \(y_{2}\text{.}\) To do so, we “cross-multiply” the system to get
    \begin{equation*} 4y_{1}y^\prime_{1} = -4y_{2}y^\prime_{2} \quad\text{or}\quad 4y_{1}\dd{y_{1}} = -4y_{2}\dd{y_{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So we can integrate this to get
    \begin{equation*} 2y_{1}^{2} = -2y_{2}^{2}+C\qq{or}y_{1}^{2}+y_{2}^{2} = C_{1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the equation of a circle of radius \(\sqrt{C_{1}}\text{,}\) and so every trajectory \(\vec{y}\) for this system will be a circle centered at \(\vec{0}\text{.}\) Hence \(\vec{0}\) is a center.

    Subsection Eigenvalue Criteria for Stability

    Consider the matrix ODE \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda_{1},\lambda_{2}\) denote the eigenvalues of the \(2\times 2\) matrix \(A\text{.}\) Then \(\vec{0}\) is a:
    The rule of thumb is this: the real parts of the eigenvalues determine whether a trajectory moves towards or away from the origin, and the imaginary part determines if the trajectory has a periodic/oscillating nature to it.
    We say that the origin is a stable critical point of \(\vec{y}^\prime=A\vec{y}\) if all trajectories that start “close” to \(\vec{0}\) remain close at all future times. Equivalently, it’s stable if each trajectory will eventually be contained within some circle centered at the origin as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Otherwise, we say that \(\vec{0}\) is unstable. If it so happens that every trajectory that starts close to \(\vec{0}\) tends to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{,}\) we then say that \(\vec{0}\) is a stable and attractive (or asymptotically stable) critical point. Equivalently, \(\vec{0}\) is asymptotically stable if every trajectory goes to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\)

    Example 4.3.8. Eigenvalue conditions for asymptotic stability.

    Let \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\) denote a matrix ODE where \(A\) is a constant \(2\times2\) matrix. What conditions on the eigenvalues of \(A\) will give an asymptotically stable critical point at \(\vec{y}=\vec{0}\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(\vec{y} = \vec{y}(t)\) denote a nonzero solution of the matrix ODE (and therefore a trajectory). Then in order for \(\vec{0}\) to be asymptotically stable, we need \(\vec{y}\to\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda_{1},\lambda_{2}\) denote the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) Then \(\vec{y}\) will have the form
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y} = c_{1}e^{\lambda_{1}t}\vec{y}_{1}+c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\vec{y}_{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The previous paragraph shows that \(\vec{y}\) must go to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\) if either \(c_{1}=c_{2}=0\) or if each exponential goes to \(0\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Since we assume \(\vec{y}\neq\vec{0}\text{,}\) this means we need \(e^{\lambda_{i}t}\to0\) for \(i=1,2\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) This implies that the real part of each eigenvalue must be negative, because the real part of each eigenvalue is what determines the growth of \(e^{\lambda_{i}t}\text{:}\) if \(\lambda_{i} = a+bi\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} e^{\lambda_{i}t} = e^{at}[A\cos bt+B\sin bt]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So \(\vec{0}\) is asymptotically stable if the real parts of both eigenvalues are negative.
    By a similar argument to that used in Example 4.3.8, we can say that \(\vec{0}\) is stable as long as the real part of each eigenvalue is no greater than \(0\text{.}\) Likewise, \(\vec{0}\) is unstable if the real part of any eigenvalue is positive.

    Example 4.3.9. Long term behavior of a system of interconnected tanks.

    Two tanks \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) containing 200 gal each of a water-salt mixture are set up as follows:
    • Tank 1: Pure water flows in at 12 gal⁄min and solution from Tank 2 flows in at 4 gal⁄min; solution also flows out of Tank 1 and into Tank 2 at 16 gal⁄min.
    • Solution from Tank 1 flows in at 16 gal⁄min; solution flows out of Tank 2 and into Tank 1 at 4 gal⁄min, and solution is emptied from Tank 2 at an addition rate of 12 gal⁄min
    Will the salt eventually empty from both tanks?
    Solution.
    Let \(y_{1}(t)\) denote the amount of salt (in pounds) in Tank 1 at time \(t\) (in minutes), and let \(y_{2}(t)\) do the same for Tank 2. Then
    \begin{align*} y^\prime_{1} \amp = 4\frac{y_{2}}{200} - 16\frac{y_{1}}{200}\\ y^\prime_{2} \amp = 16\frac{y_{1}}{200} - 16\frac{y_{2}}{200}\text{.} \end{align*}
    This system is equivalent to the matrix ODE \(\vec{y}^\prime = A\vec{y}\) where
    \begin{equation*} \vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}y_{1}\\y_{2}\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{and}\quad A = \begin{bmatrix}-\frac{2}{25} \amp \frac{1}{50} \\ \frac{2}{25} \amp -\frac{2}{25}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We need to determine the long-term behavior of solutions of this ODE, which is itself determined by the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)
    The eigenvalues of \(A\) are
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_{1} = -\frac{1}{25}\quad\text{and}\quad\lambda_{2} = -\frac{3}{25}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since both eigenvalues have negative real part, it follows that \(\vec{0}\) is an asymptotically stable critical point of \(\vec{y}^\prime=A\vec{y}\text{.}\) Therefore every trajectory \(\vec{y}\to\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) So no matter how much salt is initially in the tanks, the amount of salt will always go to \(0\text{.}\)
